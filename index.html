1. Introduction

1.1 The Problem

Managing a chronic health condition is not a continuous, daily effort that spans multiple responsibilities:

  • Remembering and taking medications on different schedules throughout the day
  • Tracking what has been taken, missed, or skipped, and communicating that accurately to medical professionals
  • Managing appointments
  • Understanding health information that is often written in complex medical language

For many users, this administrative load sits on top of the condition itself. Someone with ADHD may struggle with the routine and self-regulation that medication adherence requires. An older adult with dyslexia may be unable to read the information printed on a medication leaflet. A caretaker managing medications for a family member with severe ASD has no margin for error and no reliable system to fall back on.

Existing tools rarely account for this complexity. Generic reminder apps are not built around multi-medication schedules. Patient portals are text-heavy and difficult to navigate. Paper-based systems get lost and provide no safety net when a dose is missed. The result is a significant gap between what people need to manage their health and what available tools can provide.

1.2 The Solution

HealthApp is a mobile health management application designed to close this gap. It focuses on five core capabilities:

FeatureDescription
Medication managementAdd and manage medications, dosages, and schedules in one place
Medication remindersPersistent notifications that remain visible until the dose is logged
Usage trackingA clear log of every dose with timestamps
Appointment managementStore, view, and receive reminders for upcoming healthcare appointments
Healthcare communicationIn-app messaging with medical professionals, replacing phone calls

The app is built from the outset for users who face accessibility barriers. This means prioritising:

  • Plain language and simple sentences throughout the interface
  • Audio alternatives to all text content
  • Adjustable contrast and text size
  • Descriptive buttons and consistent, simple layouts
  • Icons alongside text so users can navigate without relying on reading alone

1.3 Target Users

HealthApp serves three broad user groups:

  1. Individuals managing their own chronic conditions — people with ADHD, ASD, migraines, dyslexia, hearing impairment, or visual impairment who need an app that works with their access needs rather than against them.
  2. Older adults — users who are less digitally confident but highly motivated to manage their health independently, and who may face barriers including hearing loss, reading difficulty, or unfamiliarity with app conventions.
  3. Caretakers — family members or carers who manage medications and appointments on behalf of someone who cannot do so themselves.

2. User Research

To understand the needs, challenges, and behaviours of our target users, we used two complementary research methods: surveys and semi-structured interviews. Our approach was designed to be accessible and multi-channel, since the target population faces significant barriers with standard digital research tools.

2.1 Surveys

Surveys allowed us to collect structured data from a broad range of participants. Given that our target users include older adults and people with significant accessibility needs, both the survey design and distribution had to be accessible themselves.

Distribution channels

  • Television and newspaper advertisements — to reach older adults managing long-term conditions or caring for someone who is
  • Online text-based surveys — shared via health forums, carer networks, and GP surgery websites
  • Phone-based surveys — for participants not comfortable with digital tools, or who have visual or motor impairments
  • Caretakers — explicitly invited to complete surveys on behalf of the people they support, as they often have deep firsthand knowledge of their patient's needs

Survey design

The survey included three types of questions:

  • Spectrum answers — for measuring frequency, satisfaction, and difficulty
  • Multiple choice — for demographics, conditions, device usage, and tool preferences
  • Optional open-text or voice-input responses — for participants who wanted to describe their experience in more detail

Topics covered: medication frequency, current health management tools, pain points with existing systems, preferred notification types, device usage, accessibility needs, and whether the respondent was a patient or caretaker.

2.2 Interviews

Semi-structured interviews (30–45 minutes) provided depth that surveys alone cannot capture.

Interviews were offered via:

  • Video call (with captions enabled)
  • Phone
  • In-person

Participants included people managing two or more chronic conditions, caretakers, and older adults with lower digital confidence. Key topics covered:

  • Daily health management tasks and routines
  • Experiences with missed medications or forgotten appointments
  • Challenges communicating with GPs or specialists
  • Current tools used (apps, paper charts, reminders) and their shortcomings
  • Early reactions to HealthApp design concepts

2.3 Survey Results

We received 12 survey responses. Below is a summary of key findings.

Participant demographics

CategoryBreakdown
Total responses12
Responding as patient8 (67%)
Responding as caretaker4 (33%)
Age range22–74
Conditions representedADHD, ASD, Type 2 Diabetes, Epilepsy, Migraines, Dyslexia, Hearing impairment, Hypertension

Medication management

  • 10 of 12 respondents (83%) take medication daily
  • 7 of 12 (58%) manage two or more medications on different schedules
  • 9 of 12 (75%) reported missing at least one dose per week
  • The most common reason for missed doses was forgetting (10 respondents), followed by confusing schedules (5) and being away from home (4)

Current tools

  • 5 respondents relied on phone alarms as their primary reminder method
  • 4 used paper charts or notebooks
  • 2 used a health app (neither was satisfied with it)
  • 1 relied on a caretaker's memory alone

Pain points with existing tools

  • "Alarms are easy to dismiss and I forget what they were for" (3 respondents)
  • "The app I tried had too many features and tiny text" (2 respondents)
  • "I can't read the medication leaflets. The print is too small" (2 respondents)
  • "I have no way to confirm later whether I actually took my morning dose" (4 respondents)
  • "Calling the GP is difficult with my hearing." (2 respondents)

Appointment management

  • 6 of 12 (50%) had missed or nearly missed an appointment in the past six months
  • The most common reasons: forgetting the date (5), not reading the appointment letter in time (3), and confusing details across multiple specialists (3)
  • 8 of 12 (67%) said they would prefer to receive appointment reminders through an app rather than by letter or phone call

Communication preferences

  • 9 of 12 (75%) said they would prefer text-based messaging with their GP over phone calls
  • Reasons included: hearing difficulty (3), phone anxiety (3), wanting a written record (2), and finding it easier to explain symptoms in writing (4)

2.4 Interview Highlights

We conducted 4 in-depth interviews. Below are key quotes and observations.

"I set three alarms for my tablets and I still miss them. By the third alarm I've convinced myself I already took them." — Participant A, 29, ADHD & migraines
"My son can't tell me if he's feeling unwell. I track everything in a notebook, but when we see his neurologist I can never find the right page." — Participant B, 48, caretaker for son with ASD & epilepsy
"I'd love to message my GP instead of phoning. By the time I get through the phone menu I've forgotten what I wanted to say." — Participant C, 71, dyslexia & hearing impairment
"Every health app I've tried looks like it was designed for a 25-year-old with perfect vision. I just want big buttons and plain English." — Participant D, 66, Type 2 diabetes & hypertension

Common themes across interviews:

  • Alarm fatigue — reminders that are too easy to dismiss stop working within weeks
  • Fragmented records — medication logs, appointment letters, and GP communication are spread across multiple systems
  • Accessibility as a prerequisite, not a feature — participants did not want an "accessible mode" buried in settings; they needed the default experience to work for them
  • Caretaker burden — caretakers described managing health admin as a second job with no reliable tools
  • Phone call avoidance — multiple participants described avoiding contact with their GP because the only option was a phone call

2.5 Synthesis

The research confirmed three core design priorities for HealthApp:

  1. Reminders must be persistent and hard to ignore — standard alarms are not effective for this population
  2. Logging a dose must be achievable in one tap — any additional steps lead to incomplete records
  3. Text-based GP communication is essential — phone calls are a barrier for the majority of target users
